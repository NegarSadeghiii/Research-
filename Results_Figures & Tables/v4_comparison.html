🏥 Urgency Tier Analysis — 50 Patients Split into Three Groups
Important note on the "All 50 patients appear late" result: The model contains a variable called LATE[p] (lateness for each patient p). Because this variable does not appear in the objective function (we minimise cost only), the solver is free to set it to any value within the allowed range — even when the patient is actually on time. This makes the raw count of 50/50 misleading.

The correct way to measure deadline compliance is to directly compare actual turnaround time against the due date: only 35 out of 50 patients genuinely exceeded their due date (all high-urgency and all medium-urgency patients). However, none of the 50 patients violated their effective deadline (due date + maximum tolerated lateness), which is the hard constraint that must hold. The low-urgency group (15 patients) actually finished one day ahead of their due date.

Feasibility Guarantee — Why the Model Will Always Have a Valid Solution
The feasibility rule: The model is guaranteed to have at least one valid solution as long as every patient's effective deadline (due date + maximum allowed lateness) is at least as large as the minimum physically achievable turnaround time.

The minimum possible turnaround time is: 1 day (air outbound) + 7 days (manufacturing) + 7 days (quality control) + 1 day (air inbound) = 16 days. All three urgency tiers satisfy this condition.

Recommended fix for the LATE variable display issue: Add a tiny coefficient on lateness in the objective function — for example, total cost + 0.001 × (sum of all patient lateness values). This costs virtually nothing but forces the solver to minimise lateness to its true value (zero when a patient is on time), so the reported lateness figures will be accurate.
